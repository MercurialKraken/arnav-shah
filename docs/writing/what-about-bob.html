What About Bob

by Arnav Shah

S.O. 1984. On The Dyson Sphere.

Bill, the Chief of the Backburn Unit (you know, the infamous Irrelevant Cases Unit: Rebranded), zapped awake. Literally. He was induced with a 3-mA alternating current by his thermally regulated sleeping pod. These system firmware updates kept getting increasingly annoying. Some dumbass graduate student at the Academy had figured that a medically approved electrical discharge into the human body had the highest efficiency of morning responsiveness. Bill hated it.

“Good morning, William,” Bill’s home-installed PA system trilled. Everyone had one of these now.

“Norm Score down 0.3 points from yesterday. This change is likely due to your non-responsiveness to the mandatory community engagement survey. Warning: If your Norm Score continues to drop, you will be reassigned.”

He groaned, rolling out of the hibernation gel. He ignored the system.

He could hear the whirring of the dispensary unit preparing his breakfast. Still in a stupor, he walked through his bathroom and leaned over the laser-basin-unit, staring at his reflection in the mirror as the system went about flossing Bill’s molars.

He planted himself down onto the sanitary hemisphere and pulled up his feed. His vision of the chrome walls blinked out as his neuro-optical implants replaced it with a mass of content. Bill moved to his trending page. Yes, he ‘wasted’ his time watching brainless content. No, he wasn’t obsessed, he just found this to be his means of escapism. Today, his trending page showed him a beautiful clip of the Sphere taken from the solar-facing hull. Bill saw the beauty of nature. The calmness of the void torn through by a ball of fire felt poetic. The Sun reached for the body of the sphere, flaring outwards as the decomposer ring emptied processed waste into the star. He found the serene thrumming noises of the void oddly calming.

Yet, he couldn’t help but be a little confused. Clips like these were rare – high grade thermal cameras, rad proofed drones, and surveillance capture permits didn’t come cheap. But, this topping the trending list means either the NormNet is having a slow day or he’s missing something. Bill expands the comment section from the top right of his vision.

Gorgorex19: “goddamnn IR 300,300,300 [crying face] #JusticeForBob”

Bill blinked twice, and his opticals obeyed, switching to the IR band. The playback shifted. The beautiful glow faded into reds and blues. He panned away, to the coordinate Gorgorex19 mentioned. At first, just static. Then, he saw a red silhouette. Bipedal. Walking calmly towards one of the structures of what was likely the decomposer ring array. Stopping just short of the gravitational red zone. And then, with no warning, the figure was ripped inwards. And SHRED.

A flash of orange. Strings of yellow were dispelled out into the inferno below reminiscent of cheese being grated over enchiladas. The feed cut out. Bill let out a breath he didn’t know he’d been holding.

“Well, shit,” Bill whistled as the bowl’s pressurized air barraged his ass clean.

***

“Good Morning, Spheroids. For those of you with a massive hangover from last night’s Circle Day festivities, today is SO 1984.”

Bill sipped his synthbrew, vaguely paying attention to Pheme’s Morning Show. Still jarred by the horrific video-clip.

“An unfortunate start to the morning for some. A janitor titled Bob fell through the solar shredder in Clean’s disposal room, and his entrails littered the sun. This scene was caught entirely on streamer bignance2’s otherwise stunning wide-spectrum capture, caught quickly by you jobless lot in the comments section.” Pheme continued.

“The leading theory is that CleanSphere had a leaky gravitational insulation around their Hercules Shredder Unit 1000. This caused the solar gravity to act beyond the red zone delineation and yank Bob through. There’s a whole bunch of other theories though, but given the post-Privacy Wars visual capture regulations, Schrodinger’s Bob is as alive as he is dead.”

“Huh,” Bill grunted in agreement. He was frustrated at the brutally careless attitude of society. A man has just died. Show some respect and discretion for his loved ones. He spiralled into thought again, wondering how heartbreaking it would be for his family – if he had one – to hear about his death. To watch it happen in excruciating detail on the Net.

“We have just received information that the Backburn Unit has been assigned the case and are doing their best to figure out just what the fuck happened.”

As if on cue, Bill received a ping notifying him of being assigned the Bob case. Bill sighed. Backburn was his unit. To be fair, calling it a unit was an being generous. It was really just him, a bunch of interns, and his assistant Watson, who really wasn’t much more than an intern himself. His job was to “deal with” cases that didn’t really matter. He abhorred it. But he wouldn’t have anyone else do the job. To Bill, these cases were his raison d’etre.

“In other news, we have with us today a survivor of the Pirates’ attack last month, Janitor Bot 9974. So, Janitor Bot 9974, welcome to the Morning Show. Tell us, how has your recovery process been…?”

Bill vaguely recollected the Pirates’ corruption of the Janitor Bots, ending in them spitting obscene curses at anyone they see. Eventually, they had insulted a Top 100 (some Foothelix player), who expressed his dissent and caused the manufacturing company to lose enough Trust Value to tank its Norm Score and go bankrupt. After that, the Janitor Bots occasionally kept popping up and causing a nuisance, but apparently some of the survivors had been in rehabilitation for a while.

“Hi, Pheme, you can call me JB. Yes, our family has been in therapy for a while now, you void-licking spark suckers. Our shrink says we have been getting better but she’s a meteor-eating shitstick. So what would she know?”

Bill nearly choked on his caffeine. The bot looked too human. Same corporate reasoning as always: make them ‘relatable’. Bill found it creepy. And, what fanned his ire at society more was the fact that no one seemed to question why therapy was more available to these robots than to the humans like Bob.

Conversation went on like this for a bit until Pheme switched to the break where some academy-student was advertising their startup that made Sanitizing Autonomous Drones. He claimed that SAD would be impenetrable to malicious and corruptive attacks and was also equipped with next-generation gravitational contour detection, which would let it avoid the gravitational pull of leaky solar shredders, amongst other things. Wow, Bill rolled his eyes. That was quick.

“The Bob incident has already impacted the trust rating of CleanSphere, with some speculating that JanitoTech may finally dethrone their monopoly on janitorial services. The market is watching closely.”

The feed split into two. A trust rating analyst and a leaderboard expert started arguing over predicted movement.

Bill slurped the rest of his synthbrew.

He hated the leaderboard system.

He hated that it governed everything. Every day, the system published a new leaderboard based on Norm Scores. A Spheroid’s Norm Score (NS) was everything, from currency to a measure of popularity. Even organizations and groups had their own scores. It boiled down to a secret formula of Trust, Utility, and Impact.

No one really knew how the NS was actually calculated. The system just did it. The Norm basically serves as a quasi-religious doctrine. One can only access public services, transit, housing, jobs if their Score is high enough. Their NS decides their employability and consequent job assignment every quarter.

Bill had seen what this stupid metric could do to people. Lives were not supposed to be a game.

***

Bill stared out into the distance. The hexagonal panel below him thrummed. He stood, silently urging the kinetic walkway to move faster even though he knew he couldn’t do anything about it. Still, out of some sadistic sense of discipline, Bill walked in-place, convincing himself he still had a choice.

Every wall around him pulsed with a subtle glow. Audiovisual lensing had empowered advertisers to take targeted marketing to a new extent. Everyone saw something different on them, hearing only what they wanted to.

He passed a wall showing a Top 10000 NS influencer, babyRhino100 in tears. The text #JusticeForBob: I didn’t know him personally, but I feel like I’ve lost a part of myself overlay the crying girl.

A woman to his right burst out in tears.

Out of pure concern and a little bit of a saviour complex, Bill asked her “What are they showing you?”

“Bob. He’s on everyone’s feed today. The talk of the Sphere,” she replied, wiping her face on her sleeve.

“Did you know him?”

“Who?”

“Bob.”

“Oh. No. But he didn’t deserve that. A true hero.” And then she completely broke down.

Bill stared ahead, lips thinning into a line.

The walls seemed to shift around him. This time, he saw a blazing hologram of a masked figure. A Pirate. The figure raged.

“Another body fed to the Sun! Another worker lost! We refuse to stay silent. CleanSphere must burn. Fight for justice. Fight for yourselves.”

The Pirates were a group of far-left aggressors that claim to be a peaceful Union, but really there was talk of branding them as a terrorist organization. Recently, they were getting bolder. Bill felt that they had been given too much freedom to cause havoc. The Sphere did need to get something done lest someone really get hurt.

The feed changed again. This time Bill saw a full-on segment from the Court of Ellipses, one of the fringe cult-orgs that had risen over the last decade or so. A bearded man with glowing eyes and a spinning elliptical halo declared,

“Bob was a construct. A simulation. He never existed. What you saw was a playback error. The higher-ups will try taking this down. But beware. The Sphere tests us.”

Bill rubbed his temple. The cults always got louder when society was confused. They were the noise filling the void. People would cling to any narrative like it was oxygen.

A nearby ad flickered. “Visit the Bob Memorial Experience – Now Open. Touch the Mop and Hear his Story!” The Holo showed a smiling janitor taken from stock footage smiling and waving.

Bill closed his eyes and muted his auditory implant. Society would really jump at any opportunity to make themselves more relevant. A man has literally been fucking grated.

The road moved him into the Public Safety Ministry hub’s first floor, where the Backburn Unit was housed. The front glass parted, scanning his iris.

Inside, Watson was bouncing with barely contained excitement.

“You’re here!” Watson beamed, his assistant badge shimmering with low-rank pride. “I’ve been running trend analysis since the morning. We’re getting huge attention metrics. If we solve this, like really solve it, our Trust Value could spike double digits.”

Bill grunted. “Mm.”

Watson didn’t seem to notice the lack of enthusiasm. “The relevance timer just hit 16 hours. We’ve got to move.”

Well, Bill thought, this would all be over in 16 hours, after which, like every other case, this one would be memory-holed and tagged as concluded.

Watson continued, “I went ahead and scheduled a holocall with a CleanSphere rep.”

Bill raised an eyebrow. “Let me guess, room 4?”

Watson grinned. “They’re already projecting.”

“Perfect. This is why I keep you around Watson. You keep dragging me into situations I’d rather die than be in.”

The doors to room 4 dinged open.

Inside, a pristine hologram of a woman stood at attention, hands clasped behind her back, heels together, and jaw locked in poise.

Her badge read ‘Lindsey | CleanSphere Public Relations’

Bill stepped in. Watson tailing behind.

“Alright”, he said. “Let’s get this circus started.”

***

“Mr. William.” Lindsey nodded.

Bill sat on the floating disk. “Lindsey. Why don’t we cut to the chase. You give me your prepared statement, and then you can answer my questions.”

“Ahem,” Lindsey cleared her throat. “Firstly, CleanSphere would like to express its sincerest condolences on the tragic loss of employee #808.

Bill narrowed his eyes. “Bob.”

Lindsey paused, before recovering. “Yes, of course. Bob. Bob was subject to an unfortunate gravitational anomaly that affected the structural containment of the Hercules Shredder Unit 1000. Despite safety protocols, the system suffered a leak event at 03:14 LC (Local Cycle), resulting in the breach. We are currently working with internal audit teams to file for appropriate deferment under clause 43.B of Workplace Incident Accountability.”

“Convenient timing for deferment claims.”

“The leak had been reported days in advance to sphere authorities, but we had neither received authorization to temporarily shut down the decomposer ring nor procure a replacement unit. It was alas a matter of bureaucracy.”

“Bureaucracy killed Bob?”

“And that is truly a misfortune we express utmost sadness about,” she said dryly.

“You said employee #808. Was that Bob’s digitag?” Watson chimed in.

“Yes. That is what was retrieved from the logs.”

“Interesting,” hummed Watson, “because the logs don’t show any biometric data for Bob after cycle 02:59. Fifteen sub-cycles before the incident. Why?”

Bill raised an eyebrow at Watson, who simply shrugged. Watson was a smart kid with a bright future beyond the Backburn Unit. He was diligent, and evidently, he’d been through the logs already.

Lindsey smiled tightly. “We are still confirming which biometric logs were corrupted due to the grav-flux spike. It likely scrambled-”

Bill caught it.

“There was no grav-flux at 02:59.”

Another pause.

“Well,” she recalibrated, “while there was no biometric data, we placed his RFID tag at the location of the incident. And this is without question.”

“No chance of error or misassignment?” Watson asks.

“Impossible. Tags are gene-locked and monitored by our biometric trackers.”

Watson frowned. “But the biometrics cut out before the incident. Can you say for sure that this was Bob’s tag? I’m no expert in physics, but, if there was a leak, solar electromagnetics could cause interference with your RFID system, right? What about surveillance footage? Surely, CleanSphere has surveillance footage.”

“Unfortunately, no. There was no internal footage. This incident occurred in one of our newer facilities. We were still waiting for our surveillance permits to come through.”

Bill noticed her dodging the question.

Bill snapped. “So let me get this straight. You don’t even know for certain that it was Bob. All you know is that a tag showed up at the scene. You know someone got pulled into the shredder. But you don’t have biometric or visual identification of this entity. All you have is a badge that we can’t even rely on because of electromagnetic interference.”

Another pause.

Lindsey’s voice was firm. “The RFID badge is enough for incident classification. Does it really matter whose badge it was?”

Bill was stunned. “Yes. It does. Bob was a person. A person who had to get blended with the fucking Sun before someone noticed he existed. And even then, no one even thought to check whether it was even him!”

“Mr. William, if you are dissatisfied with CleanSphere’s internal handling, you are welcome to escalate this to the Trust Oversight Tribunal. I refuse to answer any more questions. Good luck with your investigation Mr. William.”

With that, her projection winked out.

Watson had stayed quiet through Bill’s loss of control.

“Well,” he offered weakly, “that was unhelpful.”

Bill exhaled. “Actually Watson, good work.”

Watson blinked, “but she really told us nothing that would help us. Unless… You really think it wasn’t him?”

“I think CleanSphere doesn’t care who it was. That alone makes it our job to find out.”

“And how are we going to do that. She said there was no footage.”

“When a company like CleanSphere is locked in corporate drama with another big name like JanitoTech, there is always footage. Maybe not legal footage. And if there is footage, it’s almost certainly being peddled in the Underbelly.”

A memory teased faintly at his head. Of a girl. A boy. A purple knife. Bill had a strange relationship with the Underbelly.

“That’s on top of the blacklist. Just walking in there would tank your Norm Score.”

Bill shrugged, “Mine’s doing that by itself anyways. But, if there is a tape out for sale in the Underbelly, we have to get to it before the CleanSphere guys do.”

“Why go through all this risk for a standard case like this?” Watson hit back.

Bill stared at him. “The others think we sit here and make a show of solving these irrelevant cases to appease the public. No. We do this because even the irrelevant cases deserve closure. No one deserves to be overlooked by society. Neither in life, nor in death. Now, we can’t do much about their life, but we sure as shit can do our best to make sure this doesn’t just happen to the janitor that replaces Bob.”

Bill started walking. “Get your coat Watson. We’re going to see what they don’t show on the trending pages.”

***

The road ended three levels above the Underbelly. Anything closer and the cameras would log you and your Trust Score would swan dive. So, Bill and Watson took the stairs. The oily, sticky, ill-maintained stairs. Disgusting.

The first thing Watson noticed about the Underbelly was the screens. Five story high screens spanned floor to ceiling. Here, there was no lensing, the screens showed everyone the same feed. ‘Underground Robot Fights, now family friendly’ looped in lurid slow motion over a clip of chrome torsos hugging, then ripping the arms off each other to the applause of a cartoon host.

A vendor somewhere sat on the conveyor-less street, fanning vials of purple and cyan into a peacock tail. “The Top Thousand Feeling for a night,” he offered, smiling through shimmering teeth. Next to him, another demoed an appearance mod implant by blooming fins along her jawline and proceeding to fold them back into dimples. Watson found this highly unnerving.

“Eyes forward.” Bill muttered.

Watson nodded, hiding his hands in his coat.

Bill walked forward, right into a store – which was more like a hollowed-out enclosure into the hull of the Sphere – named The Den. Inside, blasters adorned every surface. A woman lounged over a workbench as if it was her throne. Her head was shaved, framed by cheekbones that looked sharper than cheap plasma knives. What stood out about her though was her tattoos, encasing her from her fingers to her neck in digital ink. The tattoos were a living machine, interlocking fractals of gears that rotated just beneath the skin, teeth catching, slipping, and catching again. Myth made fashionable, thought Watson. Gears hadn’t existed since the Hub monopolized power transmission.

“Bill. So, I take it you’re still chasing ghosts for the Backburn Unit? I would’ve thought by now you would have realised that you can’t really profit off the dead… only the dying… but besides,” she smiled. “What can I get you boys today?”

“The usual,” said Bill initiating a 10 Norm Point transfer.

Watson raised an eyebrow.

The tattoo lady brought out a synthetic print plastic-looking handgun, “Word on the street’s Jolgun’s got a tape. Claims it’s from the decomposer ring itself. He’s been running his mouth about it and some deal with the Pirates all night.”

“What’s that?” asked Watson, eyeing the blaster with an apprehensiveness that characterized this whole place.

The lady teeth glinted, “This, my friend, is a Java-34 print two-shot disposable conceal-carry hand-blaster.”

Watson gawked.

When they stepped back onto the street, Watson couldn’t hold it. “You just spent a thousand points – half your monthly allotment – on a disposable blaster?”

“Nope. I spent it on the information,” Bill said.

“Then what’s with the blaster? Our department isn’t even allowed to carry anything more dangerous than a P-Knife.”

Bill glanced at him, voice low, “Because people like her won’t survive too long if they look like rats. If I walked out empty handed, someone would notice. Then someone else would connect the dots. She’s doing us a favor, let’s show her a little kindness. And as for the permissibility… it’s disposable. I’ll dispose of it when we get back.”

***

They found Jolgun two streets down. His setup was impossible to miss primarily because it was all analog: piles of tapes spilling out of an old woven basket, likely older than much else in the Sphere. The video pins had handwritten labels affixed to them. Some were unauthorized surveillance and the others were porn, officially making this the most illegal basket in the Spheres.

Bill told Watson to stay back and he approached Jolgun.

Jolgun looked up. “Looking for anything specific?”

“New drops from any of the majors,” Bill said. “CleanSphere, if you’ve got it.”

Jolgun started digging through the pile, muttering under his breath. “Got a few frames from their ring feeds. Not sure what’s on ‘em. It’s going to cost you though.”

He slid a few pins across the table, into the player, one by one, letting Bill preview short loops. Most of them were static, still images with humming noises in the background, and then, a face. Bob.

Bill’s pulse jumped. “Stop. That one.”

“Not for sale,” Jolgun said immediately moving to snatch the pin back from the player.

“I’ll pay double.”

Before Jolgun could respond, a blur on a hoverboard sliced between them, ripping the player from his hands. Red emblem. Pirate.

“Watson! Move!”

The Pirate shot down the aisle, cloak snapping behind like torn flame. Bill lunged after him, lungs burning, boots clanging against the ferrocrete. Ahead was the tunnel to the public concourse, withing surveillance zone. If the Pirate reached that, the feed would auto-flag and vanish forever.

“Stop!” Bill shouted. The Pirate didn’t even look back.

Bill drew the Java-34, thumbed the safety off. He fired.

A yellow bolt carved the air, hissed past the Pirate’s shoulder, and burst into sparks against a vending drone. The Pirate swerved, crashing through a stall of mechanical doves. Wings detonated into the air, metal feathers rained.

Watson froze, trying to calm the vendor. Bill didn’t stop.

He vaulted over the debris and plunged into the alley behind the stall. The light died there. The hum of the Sphere pressed close, hot and alive. He slowed, senses crawling. Something about this darkness felt artificial in the otherwise overwhelmingly bright Underbelly.

Switching bands, his vision bled into infrared. The walls glowed dull red. Nothing. Then, a blinking black dot above.

Super-cooled magnetics. Electromagnetic boots.

He’s above me.

Bill jerked sideways just as the Pirate dropped like a thunderbolt. A plasma blade flashed red. The heat scorched Bill’s cheek. He blocked with his own knife, the clash sizzling into the air. Sparks danced across the walls.

The Pirate moved as if gravity was but a construct, dancing from wall to floor to wall, slicing in arcs that hummed through the IR haze. Bill’s breath tore ragged through his throat. His right arm trembled.

A kick. Bill stumbled back. Another slash. He parried, plasma hissing. The Pirate spun off the wall, twisting through the air, blade aimed for Bill’s throat.

Bill didn’t think. He fired.

The world exploded in yellow light. Heat slammed into him, clawing up his arm. He screamed as his skin blistered through the uniform. The Pirate hit the wall hard, sliding down until stillness found him.

Silence.

Bill looked at his hand. The blaster was fused to his palm. The skin blackened, bubbled. The air stank of melted plastic and flesh. He wanted to vomit. He laughed at the absurdity of it all, remembering the Weapons Safety Lecture that warned the squad of the likelihood of third degree burns on firing blasters in close range.

He forced his numb fingers open, pried the weapon free, and saw the video pin glinting between the Pirate’s frozen fingers. For a long moment, he just stared at it.

Then he reached down and took it.

***

Bill stepped back into the streetlight. The neon painted his burned arm in amber and magenta. The doves still fluttered overhead, mechanical wings gliding through the light.

Watson was waiting, face flushed, hands still mid-argument with the vendor. He saw Bill and stopped speaking. His eyes flicked to the scorch marks, the blaster, and the small drive clenched in Bill’s fist.

“Bill… what happened?”

“Nothing,” Bill said. His voice hollow. “It’s done.”

They walked in silence. Bill felt detached, as if walking through someone else’s life.

He thought of Bob. A janitor no one cared about until he was torn apart. A man replaced by a hashtag. And now Bill had killed someone to keep his story alive. Once again, he thought of the irony of it all.

The kinetic walkway carried them upward, out of the Underbelly’s glow. For a fleeting second, Bill imagined the Pirate opening his eyes again, red light of the katana’s plasma being unleashed. Then the image vanished, leaving only the thrum of the Sphere.

Mor had told him the Pirates would ruin his life if he came back too soon. He thought he was ready for it. He never thought he’d take a page out of their book.

***

Bill was 16 again, in Mor’s workshop, the only place where he had ever felt needed.

Sparks hissed as she tore open a flux ring, tattoos of rotating gears grinding beneath her skin.

“3.5,” Bill offered, already holding out the wrench.

Mor didn’t look up. She took it.

Every screen that night in the Underbelly outside the shop showed Sy – the Underbelly kid who saved someone rich and saw astronomical rises in NS.

For twelve hours, he was Spherehead.

When they learned where he was from, his Norm Score fell sub-zero.

By morning, he was dead.

Mor snorted, yanking Bill out of his daze. “I give it a week.”

Before Bill could jump to Sy’s defence, Roi stumbled in wearing Pirates red, pupils blown wide. Bill looked at Mor.

“Go,” said Mor to Bill, “have some fun today.”

“You got it?” she whispered to Bill as they left the Den, grinning too hard.

Bill hesitated. Mor had one rule: No politics.

But he never really could say no to Roi.

He pulled out the metallic cylinder from his pocket. Roi snatched it and slid her P-Knife’s hilt into the cylinder. The red glow bled into purple, the heat tickling Bill’s nose. The knife would now have a slight grav-pull at its edge, making it cut cleaner and deeper. Bill would see this glow only once again in his life after that day.

Roi’s eyes lit up. She oohed at it. She kissed him on the cheek. “Bill, youra saviour.” Yes, that made the risk worth it.

Bill followed, beaming as Roi walked away from the Den.

***

The Underbelly Plaza was chaos that night. Sy’s face still everywhere.

Roi was arguing with another teen that Bill vaguely recognized. He’d helped Bill lift a shipment into the shop once.

Bill wasn’t sure what started it, but he heard Roi’s voice snapping, “If you’re not with the Union, you might as well try giving the intangible system a handy.” She made drunken up-and-down motions with a fisted hand.

The boy backed into the alley wall.

“Uh. Roi, maybe we should-”

Purple flared.

The boy slumped to the ground.

Bill’s stomach collapsed. He’d given her that knife. The boy was dead. He looked at Roi again… Had her smile always been so sour?

“He shudna mouthd of,” Roi slurred.

Bill ran. She’s crazy. They’re all crazy. Them and their “movement” can go to hell. He would have no part of this. His heart thumped in his chest as he skidded to a stop in front of the Sentinel kiosk.

“Emergency report!” Bill gasped. “Someone’s hurt – stabbed – he’s dying-”

The yellow light from the kiosk washed over his face, scanning him.

“Citizen William C. Your Trust Value is insufficient for immediate response. We are unable to assist you at the moment, but your report has been recorded and will be addressed within the next 26 hours. Thank you for your vigilance.”

Bill shook his head violently. “He’s stabbed! He’ll die if you don’t send a medic!”

“Thank you for your vigilance. If you have further concerns, I would be happy to connect you to an officer.”

“YES! Do that!”

“You have been added to the queue. Estimated wait time based on NS priority: 6 hours.”

“WHAT THE FUCK?! You’re not listening!”

“Please maintain civility. NS dropped by 2 points. Further violations will receive exponential punishments.”

Bill backed away. The memory of Roi with the knife hurtling towards the boy, Tim’s body.

***

A hand grabbed his shoulder.

Mor.

“You little idiot,” she hissed, “What have you done? Little Timmy’s dead… with one of MY knives in his chest. I had one rule William. ONE!”

Bill’s throat tightened. The guilt hit him like a plasma bolt faster than anything Mor hung on the walls. “Roi promised…” he swallowed. “She wasn’t sober… and-” then the tears drowned his words.

Roi softened slightly. She pulled Bill close, holding him.

“I’m sorry. I’m sorry. Fuck. I killed us, didn’t I?”

Mor sighed, “You can’t stay here. Roi will talk. The pirates will hunt you and then bury you beside Timmy.”

Bill was shook. “Where will I go Mor? Where will a cast-out Underbellyian go? I might as well try chasing shadows.”

“Up.”

“You’re just like everyone else. You just want me gone to save yourself,” Bill lashed out.

That was the only time he ever saw hurt in Mor’s eyes.

“I’m sorry, I didn’t mean-” he started.

“Think for a second before you say something stupid like that again. You can come back. Eventually. Not just the blasters on the walls, everything down here is disposable. This is the life down here. You can only fix it from up there.”

***

The first thing Bill registered was the smell of vanilla and coconuts. The supposedly comforting scent of the regenerative gel that bubbled across his arm. He still couldn’t move his arm. He tried to sit.

A hand pushed back gently against his shoulder.

“Don’t,” Watson said, “you’ve got third-degree burns.”

“They asked me where we were, where you got the unregistered weapon from and where you nearly blew off your arm,” he continued. “I told them we were chasing a lead. Not sure how much they bought.”

“Your norm score dropped. Hard.” Watson went on, “they’ve flagged you for reassignment.”

Bill breathed, unsure what to say. Did he really go through all this just to get the boot? “We’ll deal with it later,” he sighed. “Right now, let’s watch the tape. Before the relevance timer kills it too, like it does everything else.”

Watson swallowed and inserted the pin into the wall console. The infirmary lights dimmed and whiteness flooded reality.

They stood inside a janitorial closet. Uncomfortably close to what was likely Bob’s ass. Vaporizer mops adorned the walls, the air smelling faintly of citrus.

“Olfactory FX too. Didn’t realize how high the illegal production budget was,” Watson mused to himself.

Bob stepped out into a corridor. Broad shouldered. Easy gait. His boots tapped against the composite flooring. He looked painfully normal. Up ahead, a janitor bot crested the corner, linkages whirring with precision. Save the odd jitter when it’s planning algorithm hit singularity. Or maybe it was having a panic attack.

Bob and the bot walked into each other with a muted thud.

“Sorry-” said Bob, instantly stepping aside.

The bot responded with a stream of profanity so vivid Bill’s burned arm twitched. Something obscene about Bob’s lineage. Bob turned away, shaking his head, continuing down the corridor.

Bill blinked.

“Watson,” he said slowly. “Pause.”

The playback froze.

“Magnify Bob’s left shoulder. That’s where the tag usually sits.”

Watson widened the region. Bob’s suit was smooth. No RFID sticker. No digitag. Nothing.

“Shit,” Watson whispered.

“Shift focus to the bot.”

Watson adjusted the playback. The image sharpened.

And there it was, Bob’s digitag, plastered crookedly along the bot’s jawline, half peeling at the corner.

The room chilled. Even in the simulation.

Bill smirked a little. This motherfucker. Bob was just ducking his shift, “Resume.”

The bot continued down the corridor, entering a large chamber dominated by the Hercules Shredder apparatus. The room glowed pale white, fading hazard lines tracing the floor.

Without hesitation, the bot removed a plasma knife from a utility compartment. It approached the shredder’s outer casing and began cutting.

The metal hissed. Sparks danced.

Then, a fracture. A pulse.

The gravitational field surged, snapping inward.

The bot was yanked into the shredder in a single violent instant.

The feed erupted into a red-white spray. Synthetic viscera blasted across the room. Circuit-lined organs. Smart hydraulic fluid. Artificial marrow paste.

Watson staggered, turning away. “Why- why would anyone make the inside of a bot look like that?”

The playback cut.

Watson finally exhaled. “The Pirates did this. They must have corrupted the bot, forced the self-destruct. They weren’t just teaching the robots libel; they were pushing them to destruction. That’s murder. That’s-”

“They tried to retrieve the tape,” Bill finished. “Desperate. Panicked. Makes sense now.”

“And Bob… what about Bob?”

Bill’s vision flickered. Notifications.

Watson glanced at his own feed. “Bill… the leaderboard updated. There’s a new Spherehead.”

Bill didn’t open it.

Watson did. His face drained. “I think I know what happened to Bob.”

A second ping rang around the room.

Spherewide holiday declared. Effective immediately.

Bill smiled. Then he laughed. Then he roared. This time, all the Sphere’s politics, the propaganda, the misinformation, and the games, they all backfired. They made Bob a big deal. Now, the sphere was almost certainly up in flames, at least metaphorically.

And Bill loved it. For all the Sphere took from him in its ignorance, all it took was one janitor trying to avoid work to break the system. Like with Sy, it wouldn’t last. Mor was right, nothing on this Sphere ever does. No. But Bill found something in him long-subsided rising. A faint glimmer of hope that things could change. Something he hadn’t felt in a long time.
