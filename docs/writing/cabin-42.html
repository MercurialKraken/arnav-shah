Cabin 42

by Arnav Shah

The train thunders by the lush green grasslands boasted by the West, through the quarries that nestle within the Southern red hills, under the bleak overcast sky that ever sheets the North with frothing snow, while caressing the peaceful, meditative heartbeat of the sea.

Yet, through all of this, the boy sitting in cabin 42 continues to stare out the window to his right, which spans between the blood-red cushioning of the booth seating. He was bound for a place beyond the world he knew, in a direction he did not care to know.

Gregor sat in his seat—42A—wiggling his numb right foot in an attempt to get the blood circulating through there again. He should not have worn the baggy jeans today, and his go-to red sweatshirt had him profusely sweating under his collar. He didn’t care, though. He stared outside his windows as the world he knew rushed by. He was tired, exhausted, and fed up with the endless writhing just to stay afloat. He tried to remember how he had ended up here.

“Ah, yes,” he thinks, it was that last interview? Or was it the first? Well, the finer details didn’t matter; it all started with him walking up to the security officer who greeted him with an empty, half-hearted salutation outside the Blueberry Pvt. Ltd. Etc.. Office A Annex B Directive 42 Division 3. Gregor noted an ironic contrast between the security clad in black and the imposing crystalline white structure where he was required to get his brain imaged after signing forms 42, 81, and 999 at Table 3 in the Window to the right of the Fingerprinting department, just before his interview with the Vice Sub Floor Adjunct (Possibly Retired), after which he would sign form 42 again. He spent a few hours on the night before memorizing these instructions, for he was determined to get the job this time.

Excited, he was ushered into a long corridor where he was requested by the security officer with the lifeless stare. He promised himself he would never end up like that. He walked an hour down the angelic and prismatic, slightly chilly corridor, which he distinctly remembered took him just a minute to walk down the last time he was interviewed. Yet, he was able to arrive at Table 3 at exactly 6:27 p.m. Standard Time, as instructed by the messenger pigeon that cawed at him from inside the nook in his Pine tree.

The desktop computer there stared him down from behind her glasses, which he was certain would fall off any minute.

“Mr. Gregor?” she asked.

“Yes.” Gregor nodded eagerly, silently rehearsing his answers to the questions he knew he was going to be asked in a short while.

“Alright. Fill in the blanks with your first name, last name, great-grandfather’s name, computer password, body fat levels, and an item of clothing of your choice as directed.”

She handed him a crisp bundle of papers that smelled like they had just been churned fresh out of the printer.

Gregor complied, dotted all his Ts and crossed all his Is perfectly, handing the papers back to the computer at the window. The arrow pointing left on the wall suddenly lit up green. This, he knew from last time, meant he was ready to enter his interview. He read the red signage on the frosty white ceiling that asked him to walk backwards through the next corridor. He understood that this was primarily so that the neural activity scans that would be taken of him while he made his way to the interview room would be more accurate. He let out a little chuckle as he realized his rear would be his first impression on his interviewer. This time, he was ready. He was going to get himself a job on the 357th—and, in this state, legally last—application. With that thought, he entered.

The room he entered backward was surprisingly dull, a departure from the trends the rest of the building had established in his mind. He saw two chairs, one lower than the other, separated by a humming ceiling light that grew unnaturally from the floor, flickering every four-point-two seconds.

A pneumatic hiss and the sliding of the wall in front of him announced the entrance of a short, stocky man in a white three-piece suit with a nametag that read Vice Sub-Floor Adjunct (Retired). Now Gregor was beginning to get a little nervous, for a Retired interviewer was never a good sign.

“Gregor?” the man said, still glued to his virtual clipboard.

“Yes,” Gregor said. “It’s a pleasure to meet you, I’m really looking forward to this opportunity.”

“Excellent, let’s get right to it then. We will begin with internal dissonance. You have three minutes. Begin.” Through all this, the man never once looked up from the clipboard.

“What?” Gregor blinked.

“Internal dissonance,” the man repeated flatly. “Describe your greatest strengths as weaknesses and/or vice-versa.”

“Ah,” Gregor smiled a little. He had prepared for this exact question 42 hours and 42 minutes ago. He began, “Well… I suppose—”

“Time’s up.”

Gregor’s smile faltered. “But it’s only been—”

“We will now proceed to the next question. Imagine you are employed here today. Why were you let go yesterday?”

“HR wanted to conduct a performance analysis of my impact on the firm from without.” Gregor heard himself say those exact words to his bathroom mirror yesterday and grew a bit more comfortable in his sandalwood chair, confirming that this interview was now within his expectations after the curveball at the beginning.

Yet, the man never so much as nodded. He simply wrote something on his clipboard and continued, “Now for this last part, I will test your emotional calibration. When I raise my left eyebrow feel shame, and when I raise my right eyebrow express ambition.”

“Sorry—”

The left eyebrow rose.

Gregor swallowed. “I—I apologize… I—I didn’t prepare well enough.”

The right eyebrow rose.

“If you give me the chance, I could—”

The left eyebrow rose. The ceiling light flickered faster, its humming intensifying.

“I made a mistake. I shouldn’t ha—”

The right eyebrow rose. The ceiling light was getting uncomfortably bright now. The humming became a screeching wail.

“I know I can improve. I can change. Give me this opportuni—”

This time, both eyebrows rose. The light went off suddenly, the humming ceasing altogether.

“I understand,” Gregor said quietly.

The man finally looked up from his clipboard and nodded.

“Then that concludes the interview. Blueberry Pvt. Ltd. Etc. thanks you for your application and interest.”

The man removed a red slip of paper titled Form 42 (Post Denial) and handed it to Gregor along with a train ticket with no specific destination written on it.

Gregor did not speak. He did not turn around as he walked out of the hole in the wall. He did not smile at the computer at the window as he walked down the drearily long corridors whose white paint was crumbling off the walls. Gregor just kept walking, away from the interview, away from this city, and away from this life. He walked until the corridors turned into fields, which opened out into a train station. He walked until he needn’t to anymore and stared outside the window at the past he was leaving behind.

The boy’s meditative stare out through the window is only broken three times. The first is when the woman in the pink dress, wearing a yellow summer hat that clearly did not go well with her dress, walks in.

Miss Hera Ambers walks into the cabin, taking her seat—42B—opposite a shabby-looking cross-dressed boy of about 23 years old. She adjusts her sunglasses and perches her right arm on her purse like an eagle hunting for its prey. She rests her head on her left hand, which in turn rests on the seat’s armrest, and stares out the window. She sighs. She is tired, exhausted, and ready to leave her old life behind.

Hera thought back to her argument with her now ex-husband that had driven her here, on this train, barrelling to a place she did not care to know. Perhaps she shouldn’t have been so hasty to run away. She shoved the thought away. She wasn’t running away from him. Besides, he forced her hand. He, her now ex-husband, the CEO of the Blueberry Conglomerate, was a man more whose commitment to quarterlies dwarfed his commitment to the vows he made under the stars.

She clenched her fists at the memory, her hands losing color. She hadn’t overreacted. Right? She’d asked him a question any in her place would. “How could you do this? How could you stand me up, again, for a meeting? On our anniversary?”

"Look, stop being unreasonable, it was only a couple of minutes on call, nothing more to it," he’d said.

Nothing to it? She could have laughed had her heart not been stuffed in her mouth, making her feel nauseously green. "You forgot. Again. Do you even remember what today is?"

They stood on the balcony of their penthouse in Uptown. The skies were not being too kind, the threat of rain looming over their heads. The waterfront facing them lashed out at the yachts banked there.

"This is pointless, we're just going to go back and forth, you know I had a board meeting. You know they needed me," said her then husband.

She couldn’t believe that he had the gall to look confused. Recently, he’d been growing increasingly aloof. Yet, she gave him second chances galore. How she longed to have those first few months back, when he made her feel loved, when he made her feel like even gravity bent to her will. That had been a fever dream. Now, his world spun around a polished table of twelve overpaid men in tailored suits. He had grown so focused on picking his Blueberries that he had forgotten the taste of everything sweet. Hera should have seen that as a sign. The man she’d fallen for had been replaced by a statue of himself, cold and unmoving.

"No,” she spat, “I needed you!"

The clouds started coloring themselves grey, their mood turning sour with hers.

And yet, he had the audacity to say, "You’re acting like I cheated on you."

"You did. Maybe it wasn’t with another woman, but you sleep with your phone like it’s your mistress. You sleep with your phones like they’re your mistresses while I’m the damn notification you swipe away at 2AM."

He checked his watch and fixed his tie unconsciously.

“What happened?” he said it this time feigning tenderness. A liar and an actor, that’s who he was, trained to give the press what they wanted in their interviews.

“What happened? There was a time when you wrote poems about my eyes, when you would fly me to another continent for every month since we started dating. You literally even gave me the moon.” Hera held her finger up for her audience, the man who had proposed to her under the singing night with a jewelled moonrock on a ring. “What happened? Your songs turned into an endless stream of numbers you rap at your laptop, you’re unable to even drive down the road to get takeout for our anniversary. What happened? Your love wanes this stupid rock. What happened? Why don’t you tell me.”

He checks his watch. Again. “What—”

"You’re even sneaking glances at your watch. Are my words just lost in the wind? Am I just a placeholder between your strategy meetings?"

"Strategy meetings? Maybe I do need a strategy to deal with you. You’re being unfair."

Now he’d done it. Thunder roared outside, or perhaps it was from within.

"I’m being honest. Something you haven’t in a long time."

The rain streaked the glass of her enclosure. The wind moaned through the rattling of the door behind them.

"You’re being overdramatic."

His words cut deeper than an eyelash curler used wrong. His betrayal of her trust stung like flea bites after a beach vacation in the Spring. "Overdramatic? You used to tell me I was your muse. Now, all I am to you is a liability. So, don’t act all surprised that your poetry started harmonizing with a Greek chorus."

Tears cut down her face like the rain outside-only colder, heavier-and somehow, they drowned her more than the storm ever could.

“So that’s it then?” He asked, out of something more than indifference but less than resignation.

Hera turned away, not speaking a word more, for what were words worth to her anymore. She walked as far as her feet would take her, the silence between them louder than any thunderstorm. She left. No bag. No plan. No destination.

She looked back at him once, reminiscent of Orpheus in the glade. And much like Eurydice, he too disappeared, into his own corporate underworld. She didn’t turn back again. Not when the glass doors clattered shut behind her, nor when the elevator descended fifty stories like a tomb being lowered into the Earth, and neither when her Mercedes stuttered to a stop at the nearest train station, like a wedding dress riddled with cobwebs, once graceful, now ruined by time. She could barely make out Uptown’s neon blur of golds and reds turning into dull purples and slate through the rain – or perhaps they were tears. Her mascara ran down her face, smeared like she was going to war.

She wasn’t, though. It was over.

She put on her hat—one she grabbed in a hurry while leaving, clearly not matching her pink dress—hiding her face and her emotion from the world, for there was no one there that could console her.

She walked up to the nearest train as the whistle blew. A half-asleep tall man in a grey jumpsuit, likely the conductor, approached her, asking her for a ticket. She simply took off the ring that lay on her left hand’s fourth finger, dropped the rock her love had become into the conductor’s open hand, and walked in.

The conductor just nodded to her and said, “Seat 42B, Madam.” She sat on her seat, across the shoddily dressed boy, and looked to her left, through the window, at the life she left behind.

The boy’s and the woman’s introspective stares through the window to their old lives are only interrupted twice more. The next is when the gloomy security guard dressed like John Wick—if he wore a hat—walks in. The brim of his black hat obscures his eyes, his coat hanging limp from his shoulder. He moves like smoke if it had weight, his face betraying no expression.

S. I. Sephus walks into the cabin and takes his seat next to a weeping lady who had probably just had an argument with her boyfriend over something petty. Oh, how he wished those were the kind of issues he had to deal with, a candle to his grenade. He took his seat—42D—and looked through the window at the life he left behind, tired and exhausted of it all. As he looked at his past rattling by him, he let his mind fall backward, into the corridors he once sealed.

The halls had once been bright, lively, sharp, and idealistic. Eventually, they had come to feel like morgue sheets, the white lie that hides the ever-sleeping. He walked through this routine every day. First went Reception, then HR, then Legal, then the never-ending labyrinth of conference rooms. One by one, the doors locked behind him, until only he remained. It was always somehow just him who remained.

There used to be a time when the night shift used to play cards on their break, gambling with what little Blueberry Pvt. Ltd. Etc. paid them, just to light themselves a smoke, revelling in the little jokes and the small laughs. But the billion-dollar enterprise could not afford to keep minimum wage workers on their payroll, and so, one at a time, his brothers-in-arms were let go, like used paper napkins, disposable.

So, he started having to lock rooms as empty as a bottle in his hands after hours, hollow and full of echoey whispers of a past that once was. He walked the same corridors he used to call home, with its lively musical background score. That music soon became the bridal chorus to a doomed marriage, the carrot still leading the disillusioned donkey. Occasionally, he would hear a laugh, grating against his ears, out of place, like a piano in a gunfight. He was alone, the jukebox in a closed bar, full of songs no one would ever listen to.

Sephus worked 9-to-9 shifts, and then some more. The best part of his day was seeing unkempt youths bubbling to give job interviews, only to come crashing down into reality in a few hours. Everyone’s house burns down eventually. He wondered when his time would come – when he would join his mates. He questioned himself repeatedly why he was still there.

He didn’t have to wait too long. The last straw, as straw generally is, came dressed in paper.

The last night was quiet. Too quiet. The corridor stretched long, as the silence after a gunshot often does. Sephus had made his usual rounds, though there wasn’t much sense to it, for there was no one left to protect.

They didn’t fire him. They couldn’t, not really. No, they just stopped telling him things, almost as if he wasn’t locking up but was being kept locked in. They stopped assigning him duties. They even revoked his clearances. He eventually saw his memory fade from the computer’s mind.

Yet, he stayed, either out of habit or duty. Or maybe simply because there was nothing else for him but streetlights flickering over a dead-end. The lights eventually got dimmer and the music softer, then the envelope showed up next to the rack where he hung his keys, the scythe he had managed to evade until now. He didn’t need to open the envelope. He knew what it meant. He’d seen the signs. He hung up his keys in the corner of the dingy room that could barely fit even just him. He folded his coat and slung it over his shoulder, a body he did not have the strength to bury. He would keep his badge. It would remind him of who he was until they decided he wasn’t. There was nothing left here for him anymore. So, he left.

He walked out of the building, a man leaving a crime scene of one he did not commit. He left through the back door, Stairwell 3, where paint peeled like old memories. He trudged through the mud and crunched over the gravel.

He wound up at the station after his stop at the bar. The clerk at the ticketing window glanced at Sephus’s badge and simply handed him a ticket. All it said was 42D. No destination. No price. He accepted it as an executioner swung his axe, a sentence quietly carried out.

He moved, with the weight of his past that had left him, found his seat next to a crying lass and a surrendered youth, sat down on the chair that smothered him with an almost motherly affection as no one ever would. He looked outside the window, at the land that was his past – cracked, ghastly—flying by.

The three stare beyond, each running away from something in their own way. This eternal gaze into the past is only broken one more time… when the Doctor walks in.

Dr. Chelsea Faust placed her satchel in the cabinet above and took her seat in the only space left—42C—with a practiced efficiency.

She looked around at the others, mentally profiling each individual who lay around her.

“Boy in red sweatshirt. Textbook escapist. Clear signs of cognitive dissonance. Possibly unemployed. Possibly underfed. Possible poet.”

“Woman in pink. Classic hysterical bourgeois. Bad color theory. Possibly easy-to-anger.”

“Man in coat. Shell shock or PTSD. Possible burnout. Could be a ghost. Need more data to confirm.”

She pressed back further into the red cushioning, eager to get away from it all. She was tired and exhausted. Her tight updo was starting to pull at her roots, risking traction alopecia, but what did it even matter? Her simple brown dress creased over her thigh like the waves falling onto the beaten shore. She looked out through her spectacles and through the window that now separated her from the sun that was setting on her past. How had it come to this? It was not too long ago that she was conducting groundbreaking ontological research as a tenured professor. Was running away really the answer? No, she wasn’t running away. Running would imply movement, which would be misleading for she was living a still-life. No, she wasn’t running away so much as disengaging or pivoting.

She’d needed the reality check. It had come to her in the form of scattered applause and empty faces. Well, what else had she expected?

Dr. Faust had been excited before her 42-page-long presentation on thanatological escapism and the decay of self-identity. She stood under the company logo, a ring with no end. She smiled at that, recollecting from her introductory semiotics class that this was a sign of hope, of a never-ending commitment to society. Laser pointer in hand, she lunged from slide to slide with cat-like grace, deftly rehearsed countless times while highly caffeinated. She explained that runaway behavior could be algorithmically predicted by mapping a subject’s ontological activity.

She hits slide 13, “Existence: A Mathematical Fiction”. As her thought sped by, the word ‘exist’ stuck, sounding foreign, irking her like a typo on the tongue. Her voice had faltered as the man in the corner yawned, his arms almost hitting the fall-ceiling in Blueberry Pvt. Ltd.’s Conference Room 2. Someone was checking their phone. And at slide 32, she heard one man clap, perhaps out of formality, or perhaps just as her cue that her time to speak had elapsed. Her confidence wavered. All she’d wanted was a few million dollars to fund her research; it should not have been Herculean.

Someone cleared their throat in one of the seats around the hardwood table, clearly wanting to ask a question. She nodded to him, acknowledging his presence.

“Fascinating. How can this be monetized?” he asked.

Silence stretched between the walls, a resounding falsetto.

“Pardon?” says Dr. Faust, not knowing any other way to respond to that.

“The runaway prediction… Can it be leveraged to drive up quarterly estimates?”

The truth of it all hit her. How had she been so blind? “Only if you can put a price on a life.”

The man sitting at the head of the round table nodded, “Perfect. We look forward to working with you and seeing the direction your work will take this enterprise.”

She laughed short and cold. The lights behind the company logo hummed above her. The logo’s hollow circle, resembling a void, now felt like an allegory for her life.

That night, she packed her notes, deleted her slides, and purged the data from all her years of experimentation. The tragedy of academia is that one can never unknow the scaffolding they uncovered in the first place. An archaeologist would spend their entire lives naming bones only for them to decorate the walls of a museum.

She smiled at the irony of it all. She, an ontologist, evading meaning. Well, it didn’t matter anyway. Existentialism was a concept that had been lost to time, like the Sumerians’ language.

The next morning, she boarded a train, knowing neither where it came from nor what it ran after. She never uttered a single syllable, choosing to keep her thoughts and ideas to herself from then on.

Back in the cabin, Chelsea presses her temple—her tight updo yanking on her scalp, the dawn of a headache seemed likely. She looks at the others again, her earlier hypotheses now feel moronic.

She whispers to no one in particular, “We aren’t passengers. We’re exhibits in the Museum of Things the World Stopped Caring About.”

She rests her head back against the headrest that embraces her with open arms, her gaze falling outside the window. She sees her reflection for a moment before it’s swallowed by the blur of her past that sweeps by.

The party of four—the youth, the lady, the working, and the learned—sit there for a while, in silence that stretched off into the world beyond. A stillness loomed, engulfing the lives that lay within cabin 42, its exhibits reflecting on what ‘life, the universe, and everything’ mean to them with meditative peace.

The boy in the red sweatshirt shifts slightly, the feeling seeping back into his right leg. His fingers began tapping the windowsill as the landscape outside opened into a sunset over the grassy banks of the sea, a rhythm testing return.

Across from him, the woman in the pink adjusts the hat she had hated all this time. Not to hide, but to see better. Her eyes, still glassy, shift to the boy’s dancing fingers. She doesn’t smile, and yet neither does she look away.

Next to her, the man in the black coat, whose bones creak under the weight of his silence, lets out a slow breath, beginning to show symptoms of life. He closes his eyes, slowly opening them again.

The doctor who sits directly in front of him adjusts her glasses. Her legs uncrossed, a posture no longer bracing for impact. She looks out through the window once again, her reflection resolving in the glass, locking eyes with herself, and letting the subtlest of smiles seep through the stony walls.

Nothing was said. Yet, in that shared silent stillness, there was a flicker, a pulse – the barest beginning of motion.

And somehow, simultaneously, defying all natural laws, for the first time, the group really looked at each other. Each of them realising that they were truly all headed for the same destination.

Somewhere something cracked, and Hera whispered four simple words, “I hate this hat.” And that was enough.

Though no one else would understand her, the rest of them did.

Gregor’s bloodshot eyes blinked. His cheeks rolled up. And somehow, like a cookpot releasing steam, he hissed a dry chuckle through his slightly misaligned teeth.

Sephus’s rumbling synchronised with his body next. His convulsions became a booming, thunderously roaring laugh.

Chelsea, incredulous, found her lips curling upward. She removed her glasses and let out an unglamorous snort. To her, the smell of sweat, tears, and blood around her now became the melange that constituted the smell of humanity.

Slowly, this clumsy scattered coughing and cackling became a cheer, loud and free. They laughed not out of joy, but from the sheer ridiculousness of it all—their laughter, a recognition.

“What were we ever running away from?” asked someone, but everyone was thinking it.

Outside, the sun now dipped below the hills on the horizon, its light a promise the world ever clung to.

The train began to slow. The rumbling came to a stop as it pulled into a station no one had bothered to name. The screeching wheels came to a halt.

“This is the final stop,” an announcer declared, “all passengers must step off the bogey, unless they wish to make the return journey.”

For a long while, everything was still. Eventually, the train exhaled a breath of steam, its doors hissing shut, beginning its crawl back to where it had once come from. And no one had stepped off the train.

The train glided into the dusk and the horizon beyond, leaving nothing behind but wind and the echoes of laughter.
